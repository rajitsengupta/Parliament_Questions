climate change

Little to debate

Indian Parliamentarians asked over 100,000 quesions to the government in the past 10 years. Only handful of them were on climate.

Rajit Sengupta

Published July 11, 2023



As an environmental data journalist, I have been tracking the occurrences of extreme weather events in India for some time now, and this constant engagement has brought me face-to-face with many issues that surround the discussion about climate change in my country.

One of the most important parameters for gauging the importance of any issue in the public and political imagination is the discussion that any issue generates in Parliament.

The success of my data stories has often been gauged by the number of questions they generate in the Indian Parliamentary debates.

This is the reason why this project is based on a survey of the number of times environment-related questions have been asked in the Indian parliament in the last decade.

In the first hour of a sitting session of the Lok Sabha, the elected house of Parliament, elected members can raise questions about any aspect of government, and the concerned Minister is obliged to answer to the Parliament. It is an opportunity for Parliamentarians to hold the government accountable and to ensure transparency and accountability in governance.

Indian legislators have raised 133,032 questions in Parliament in the past 10 years.

Just 3.5% of these questions were for the Union Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change

Majority of the climate-related questions were on forests and wildlife (21%), pollution (16%) and performance of government schemes (15%).

There were fewer questions on crimes (3%), environmental clearances (5%), waste (8%) and climate impact (11%) .

In the past 10 years, parliamentarians in India have asked a total of 133,123 questions to the government. Of these, only 3.35%, or 4,455, were directed to the Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change.

When I further analysed the categories that were included in these 4,455 questions, it turned out that the majority (21%) were about wildlife and forests, followed by questions around environmental pollution (16%) and various government policies and schemes on climate change (15%).

Conversations about conservation projects made up 13% of the questions, and climate impact (which includes extreme weather events and various government assessments on the loss and damage due to climate change) accounted for 11%..

Conversations about Environmental Waste accounted for 8% of the questions, while questions about environmental crimes and clearances accounted for 3% and 5% of the total questions asked, respectively.

Need to change

Why is that problematic? Because sneaky environmental hazards like extreme weather events are still not understood as climate or environmental hazards.

A drastic shift in the discourse is imperative if actionable change needs to be brought about. The magnitude of the environmental problem is clearly not being understood.

There is a gross mismatch between the actual ecological disasters and the realisation or conversation about climate change that is taking place in the parliament. While protecting the forest cover and wildlife are important issues, they are not the ones causing the maximum damage to life and property.

Extreme weather events are responsible for almost a quarter of all internal displacements due to weather events globally. India in fact recorded extreme weather events on 314 or 365 days this year.

We need to shift gears and prioritise which conversations are most urgent for us as humans and for the planet.



Methodology

The questions have been scrapped from the Lok Sabha website using playwright and then clubbed under nine classifications using the column title of debate. The nine classifications are:

1) Forestry and wildlife: includes everything from afforestation drives, grassland conservation, biodiversity, wild animal conservation, and incidents of human-animal conflicts.

2) Climate policy: includes environmental schemes and policies and announcements India has made in international conferences such as the UN Conference of Parties, among others.

3) Pollution: includes air, water, and land pollution from all sources, including stubble burning, diesel cars, and industrial effluents.

4) Environmental clearances: include questions raised over no-objection certificates given to industries for various activities, including mining and others.

5) Climate impact: includes the effect global warming and unplanned urbanisation have had on the environment, such as coastal erosion and land subsidence. It also includes discussions around the loss and damage caused by extreme weather events and the findings of various government assessments on climate change impacts, such as the impact of rising temperatures on farm productivity.

6) Waste: includes discussions around the quantum and handling of all forms of waste streams, including plastic, biomedical, hazardous, and others.

7) Conservation: includes various initiatives undertaken to conserve the environment or reduce the countrys carbon footprint. It also covers topics such as corporate social responsibility and the development of eco-sensitive beaches, among others.

8) Crimes: include questions around poaching, illegal mining, and other environmental crimes.

9) Others: includes topics that could not be grouped in the above classifications, such as promotion of traditional medical knowledge, compensation given to farmers, or uptake of genetically modified crops.
